Rethinking Excess Mortality

Companion Tool - Excess Mortality Analysis

Problem Statement

Excess mortality estimates are highly sensitive to arbitrary analytical choices. In particular, different reference periods produce large variability in excess mortality estimates (Figure 1 & 2). Inaccurate estimates can mislead decisions on clinical practice, funding, health policy, and public health (Ioannidis et al., 2023).

Solution

We propose a principled approach to selecting the reference period: if mortality returned to normal by 2025, pick baselines that predict 2025 well.

Equilibrium-Selected Baseline

For each country i, we select the optimal baseline period Bi* that minimizes the root mean squared error (RMSE) when predicting mortality in the post-shock equilibrium year (2025):

Bi* = arg minB ∈ ℬi RMSE(B, 2025)

where ℬi is the set of all possible baseline windows for country i using available data up to 2019, with |B| ≥ 4 years.

Key Assumption: Age-standardized mortality rates returned to equilibrium in 2025.

The average ASMR across 22 countries shows significant excess mortality during 2020-2022, lesser excess in 2023-2024, and no excess in 2025.

Case Study: New Zealand

In the charts below we can see a dramatic difference in implied excess mortality when using a standard baseline range (2016-2019, MJ Plank) versus an equilibrium-selected range (2010-2016). The left chart implies New Zealand not only saved thousands of lives in 2020, but is continuing to save thousands of lives in 2024-2025.

The effect is not limited to the 2016-2019 baseline. All of the baseline ranges used by MJ Plank, OWID, The Economist and IFoA imply that New Zealand saved a comparable or greater number of lives in the last year, as compared to 2020. Our equilibrium-selected baseline (2010-2016) is the only one that doesn't imply significant lives saved in the last year.

Note: The cumulative traces may not exactly match the referenced studies because of methodology differences.
- OWID (Kobak&Karlinsky) derive their baselines from raw death counts, not ASMRs. We have used ASMRs to enable fair comparison between countries.
- MJ Plank uses New Zealand's 2021 population as the reference population. We have used the ESP2013 standard to enable fair comparison between countries.
- Eurostat does not calculate ASMR or excess mortality for New Zealand.

The equilibrium-selected baseline (2010-2016) excludes 2017 and 2019, years with elevated all-cause mortality (+49.5 and +46.8 per 100k respectively). Notably, the 2017 mortality peak (1604.2 per 100k) was comparable to the 2022 peak (1605.1 per 100k) (Appendix 6). Including these high-mortality years in the baseline artificially inflates expected mortality, making normal mortality in 2024-2025 imply "lives saved."

Many countries have such idiosyncrasies due to heatwaves, severe flu seasons, or natural disasters. Our method reduces the effect of these anomalies by selecting country-specific baselines that predict the post-shock return to equilibrium.
